Australia
Takes Flight

Millions of Australians board domestic flights every month — from the short hop between Melbourne and Sydney to the epic Perth–Sydney crossing. This visualisation explores where we fly, how much we pay, and how the industry has changed over three decades.

Section 1 — The Network

Australia's Domestic Air Network

Scheduled domestic flights connect 117 airports across the continent — from major capital city hubs to remote regional airstrips that serve as lifelines for outback communities. Larger orange circles mark major international-grade airports. Hover over any point for details.

Scheduled Domestic Airports

Why geography matters: Australia's vast distances make flying a necessity rather than a luxury. Perth is further from Sydney (3,284 km) than London is from Moscow.

Section 3 — Passenger Volumes

Two Decades of Turbulence

Domestic passenger numbers climbed steadily through the 2000s — then collapsed twice: first with the sudden fall of Ansett Australia in September 2001, then catastrophically during the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020. Recovery has been strong but uneven.

Total Domestic Passengers per Month — 2000 to 2026
Includes all scheduled domestic airlines. Source: BITRE Monthly Domestic Airline Activity
"At the height of COVID-19 lockdowns in 2020, domestic passenger numbers fell to levels not seen since the 1980s — fewer people were flying than in 1977."
Monthly Passenger Heatmap — 2005 to 2025
Each cell = one calendar month. Darker orange = more passengers. The pale strip in 2020–21 shows the COVID collapse clearly. Source: BITRE
How Full Are Australian Flights?
Passenger load factor = share of seats filled. The dashed line marks 80% — the industry benchmark for a profitable route. Source: BITRE Monthly Domestic Airline Activity

Section 4 — What We Pay

The Price of a Seat

BITRE tracks a monthly fare index across three cabin classes, adjusted for inflation. An index of 100 represents the 2009 baseline price. Falling index values mean fares have become cheaper in real terms — good news for budget travellers.

Domestic Fare Index by Class — 2000 to 2026 (CPI-adjusted)
Index = 100 in 2009. Shaded regions mark major disruptions. Source: BITRE Domestic Air Fares Index
Key finding: Best discount fares have fallen dramatically in real terms since 2009 — meaning budget air travel is genuinely more affordable for Australians today than it was 15 years ago, despite rising living costs overall.
When to Fly Cheap — Peak vs Winter
Best discount fare index: mid-year (Jun–Aug) vs summer (Dec–Jan). Higher index = relatively more expensive. Source: BITRE
